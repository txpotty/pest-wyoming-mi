Signs You Need Raccoon Removal Services

Recognizing the signs of a raccoon infestation early can prevent extensive damage and health risks. While they are often nocturnal, there are several indicators of their presence. If you notice any of these signs, it's time to consider professional raccoon removal services in Wyoming, MI.

Unusual Noises at Night

Raccoons are most active during the night. Listen for scratching, thumping, or chattering sounds coming from your attic, walls, or chimney. These noises often indicate that raccoons are denning or moving around within your property's structure. The sounds can vary depending on the activity; scratching can be them moving insulation, while thumping might be them walking.

Hearing these sounds, especially consistently night after night, is a strong indicator of a wildlife presence. Ignoring these noises could lead to more significant problems down the line. Identifying the location and type of noise can help our technicians determine the scope of the infestation.

Damaged Property

Raccoons are known for their destructive behavior. They can tear open garbage cans, damage gardens, chew through wood and siding, and even rip apart roofing materials to gain entry. Inspect your outdoor areas for signs of tampering or damage. Look for overturned trash cans, scattered debris, or visible chewing marks on structures.

Inside your home, check your attic for damaged insulation, chewed wires, or structural damage. Raccoons can cause significant damage to the integrity of your roof and attic space, which can lead to costly repairs if not addressed promptly. They may also create pathways within walls, damaging drywall and creating potential hazards.

Presence of Droppings and Odor

Raccoon droppings are typically dark, tubular, and contain undigested food. They often defecate in communal areas known as latrines. The presence of a raccoon latrine on your property is a clear sign of an infestation and a potential health hazard. Raccoon droppings can contain roundworm eggs, which can be harmful to humans and pets.

A strong, musty odor, especially in areas like your attic or crawl space, can also indicate the presence of raccoons or their urine and droppings. This odor can permeate your home and become quite unpleasant. Properly cleaning and sanitizing these areas after removal is crucial for eliminating the odor and mitigating health risks.

Signs of Entry Points

Inspect your home's exterior for potential entry points. Look for gaps around chimneys, damaged vents, loose shingles, or openings in the foundation. Raccoons can squeeze through surprisingly small spaces. They are also excellent climbers and may use trees or fences to access upper levels of your home.

Identifying and sealing these entry points is a critical part of preventing future infestations. Raccoons have strong paws and can chew through various materials, so any vulnerability in your home's structure can be exploited. Our technicians are skilled at identifying these potential access points during the inspection process.

Seeing Raccoons on Your Property

While primarily nocturnal, seeing raccoons on your property during the day could indicate that they are sick, injured, or that food sources are scarce at night. While not always a sign of infestation within your home, their regular presence nearby increases the likelihood of them seeking shelter inside your structure. Observing their behavior and frequency of visits can provide valuable information. Seeing multiple raccoons at once, especially during the day, is a more significant concern and warrants immediate attention.

Understanding Raccoon Behavior and Risks

Understanding the habits and potential dangers associated with raccoons is essential for effective wildlife management and ensuring the safety of your family. Raccoons are highly adaptable animals that thrive in urban and suburban environments, often coming into conflict with humans.

Nocturnal Nature

Raccoons are primarily active at night, which is why you often hear them during late hours. This nocturnal behavior makes it challenging to spot them in action unless you are specifically looking for signs. Their nighttime activity often coincides with when residents are asleep, leading to surprise encounters and potential danger.

Diet and foraging

Raccoons are omnivores with a diverse diet. They forage for food in garbage cans, gardens, and pet food left outdoors. This search for food is a primary driver for them to approach human dwellings. Securing food sources is a crucial step in deterring raccoons from your property. Properly securing trash cans with animal-proof lids is a simple yet effective measure.

Health Risks Associated with Raccoons

Raccoons can carry various diseases and parasites that can be transmitted to humans and pets. This includes rabies, a viral disease that affects the nervous system and can be fatal. While rare, human cases of rabies are serious. Raccoon droppings can also contain raccoon roundworm eggs, which can cause neurological damage if ingested.

Handling raccoon droppings or coming into direct contact with the animals without proper protection is risky. This is why professional removal and sanitation services are highly recommended. Our team uses appropriate safety gear and protocols to minimize health risks during the removal and cleanup process.

Aggression and Defense

While generally not aggressive, raccoons can become defensive if they feel threatened or cornered, especially a mother protecting her young. Approaching or attempting to handle a raccoon without professional guidance can result in bites or scratches. Maintaining a safe distance is always advisable when encountering wildlife.

It's important to teach children and pets to avoid interacting with raccoons. Keeping pets indoors at night and securing pet food can also reduce the likelihood of encounters. If you suspect a raccoon on your property is exhibiting unusual behavior during the day (stumbling, foaming at the mouth), immediately contact animal control as this could be a sign of rabies.

Preventing Future Raccoon Infestations

Effective raccoon removal goes beyond simply removing the animals; it also involves implementing preventative measures to reduce the likelihood of future infestations. Taking steps to make your property less attractive to raccoons is crucial for long-term success.

Secure Food Sources

Properly storing garbage in sturdy containers with tight-fitting lids is one of the most effective ways to deter raccoons. Avoid leaving pet food or water bowls outdoors, especially overnight. Consider harvesting fruits and vegetables from your garden as soon as they are ripe.

Compost piles can also attract raccoons. Consider using enclosed composting bins or managing your compost to minimize attractants. Removing fallen fruit from trees is another small step that can make a difference. These seemingly simple actions can significantly reduce the appeal of your property to foraging raccoons.

Seal Entry Points

Inspect your home's exterior regularly for potential entry points. Seal any gaps, cracks, or openings in your foundation, walls, roof, and chimney. Repair damaged screens and vents. Consider installing chimney caps to prevent raccoons from entering through the flue. Using sturdy materials like steel mesh can help prevent raccoons from chewing through them.

Pay close attention to areas where different building materials meet, as these are common weak spots that raccoons can exploit. Regularly checking your attic for signs of damage or entry is also a good preventative measure. Addressing even small vulnerabilities can make a big difference in keeping raccoons out.

Trim Trees and Shrubs

Trim tree branches that overhang your roof, as raccoons are excellent climbers and can use these branches to access your attic or other areas of your home. Keep shrubs and bushes trimmed away from the sides of your house, as these can provide cover and easy access for raccoons.

Landscaping can play a significant role in deterring wildlife. A well-maintained yard with trimmed vegetation makes it harder for raccoons to approach your home undetected. Consider using less attractive landscaping materials near the perimeter of your house.

Install Motion-Activated Lights or Sprinklers

Motion-activated lights or sprinklers can startle raccoons and discourage them from lingering on your property. Raccoons are generally wary of sudden changes in their environment, and these devices can be a non-lethal deterrent. Placing these near garbage cans or potential entry points can be particularly effective.

These deterrents work best as part of a multi-faceted approach to prevention. While they may not completely eliminate the risk, they can significantly reduce the appeal of your property to raccoons. Experimentation might be needed to find the most effective placement and settings for these devices.

Secure Pet Doors

If you have a pet door, ensure it is securely locked at night to prevent raccoons from entering your home. Raccoons are resourceful and can quickly learn to use pet doors to gain access to food and shelter. Considering a pet door with a security feature or one that limits access to only your pet can be a wise investment.

Even if your pet is small, a raccoon can still force its way through a pet door opening. Taking this simple precaution can prevent an unwanted visitor from entering your home overnight and potentially causing damage or creating a dangerous situation.
